Leap of faith

At the behest of Department of Education (DepEd) Secretary Sara Duterte, the education system is set to undertake adamant efforts toward bolstering military training services by reintroducing mandatory Reserve Officers' Training Corps (ROTC), an arbitrary edict that goes against its deadly flaws and fallibility unveiled by its faulty implementation years prior.

Contrary to the ideologies of the current administration, history has given prominence to the program’s infirmities. Pushing through with the compulsory implementation of ROTC hearkens back to countless documented cases of military abuses, harassment, hazing, and malfeasance among cadets that litter its reputation. Recalling the very reason for the abolishment of the program back in 2001, the murder of Mark Welson Chua following his exposé on the culture of corruption within his university’s ROTC program undergirds the ineffectiveness and lack of accountability provided by the military training endeavor, the same ill-fate that high-school students are expected to undergo regardless of their say in the matter.

Amidst perennial concerns about the poor quality of basic education, insufficient resources and facilities, and an overburdened faculty workforce, the implementation of ROTC shifts funding and capital from reforming the intractable conditions that currently plague the education system. The estimated P61.2 billion allocated for the program can fund the infrastructure for 24,480 classrooms, according to a Rappler report. Despite the excessive budget provided, clamors echo concerning the readiness to implement the program taking into consideration the availability of personnel and facilitators of the program.

Educational discourse on altering current pedagogy to the “K-10+2” system further labors the point that the current educational system is not ready to withstand the changes engendered by mandatory ROTC. The time and resources redirected toward military training should rather be expended on academic and extracurricular enrichments. Reallocating capital and equipment to enable the program’s obligatory operation would compound an already underperforming learning system, ranking last in reading comprehension and second to last in math and science in the 2019 report by the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA).

Although rising tensions both within the interior of the country and from neighboring countries continue to burgeon, following the footsteps of other countries with their respective military training programs does not fit within the needs and resources of Philippine society. Historically proven to be ineffective, the mandatory ROTC program does not occupy the same seats within the ranks of well-established, more protracted foreign military training. Instead, it begs the question of whether adolescents are expected to bear the brunt of the multipolar disorder while upholding a culture of impunity for politicians, government bureaucrats, and the voracious private sector as instruments of power within current prevalent repressive military institutions.

Underlying the superficial benefits that mandatory military training provides lay documented cases of corruption, death, and incongruous public policy. Without healthy and safe military environments for ROTC cadets, supplemented with bolstering vitality toward the National Service Training Program (NSTP) act of which the ROTC is voluntary, the future portends a big leap of faith for the proper implementation of an intrinsically faulty program.
